Podcast Setup and Equipment

I am having a lot of fun doing the Angularlicio.us Podcast. Before starting the podcast, I acquired a lot of equipment while putting together a small home studio to do some composing, editing, and mixing music tracks - I have been playing sax for most of my life. So there was not a problem with having equipment. The cards below show some of the equipment I use to create the podcast.

Equipment

These are the main tools I use. A good mic, editing software that allows for multiple tracks, and a good playback system.

  1. Microphone: I have a higher-end Audio-Technica mic, but the mic I use to create the podcast is the USB Audio-Technica AT2020. The sound quality is great and it doesn't pick up some of the ambient noise that is present where I do my recording. I have a desk mic arm and a pop filter. I have used this desktop mic for many years doing voice-overs for video and screen-presentations. It is very easy to use and portable. It is constructed of metal and is very durable.

  2. Audacity: I use Audactity. This is a free open-source tool that really has more features than I need to record the podcast voice tracks. It is simple and easy to use. It allows me to create as many tracks as I need for an episode. In addition to recording the initial tracks, I use Audacity to trim the tracks and to create a stereo track for left/right stereo audio.

  3. Cubase: Cubase is a Digital Audio Workstation (DAW) that I have used since version 4 for composing, editing, and mixing music tracks. For mixing down a new podcast, a DAW really makes it easy. Because you can have different tracks for different parts of the episode. I have a template project setup with the intro/outro. I have some default mix settings for the voice tracks that I can reuse and have a consistent mix between all of the shows. Using the template allows me to just drop the new voice track and start with the editing process.

    You probably do not need to spend the money for a high-end DAW like Cubase Pro 9. There are other Cubase versions that provide the same level of editing multiple tracks at a lower price. There are also some open-source/free DAW software projects. Do a little research and choose a DAW that meets your needs. Check out the "lite" versions of some of the more popular DAW products - they will probably be more than adequate.

  4. Playback: During the editing and mixing process, you will want to listen to the playback of the track(s). You will need at least a good pair of headphones. With most audio equipment, there are a lot of options. I think the best type of headphones are studio monitor types that allow you to hear the tracks clean without any/little modification by the headphones. I like the AKG series of headphones - they have products starting at $49 and up depending on you needs and budget.
